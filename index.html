Our Mission

Mapping the stress that shapes the blood system.

We focus on the mechanistic underpinnings of inflammatory and metabolic stress programs in human hematopoietic stem cells, and how these programs drive the risk for clonal hematopoiesis and leukemia across the human lifespan.

Using primary stem cell and leukemia samples and humanized xenotransplantation models, we aim to systematically map the cellular and molecular events that link acute and chronic inflammation to stem cell dysfunction and leukemic transformation.

By understanding how stress promotes unhealthy aging and cancer in the blood system, our goal is to discover novel cancer prevention and treatment strategies.

In acute myeloid leukemia, the most common form of adult acute leukemia, the long-term survival rate is approximately

30% 5-year survival

Therapies designed to eradicate leukemia stem cells are urgently needed.

Our Approach

From bench to bedside.

We have shown that human hematopoietic and leukemia stem cells have distinct sphingolipid and inflammatory signatures compared to their differentiated counterparts.

Our long-term goal is to translate these insights into strategies that improve hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, prevent the transition from clonal hematopoiesis to leukemia, and develop targeted therapies for acute myeloid leukemia.

Research Themes

Three lines of inquiry.

1

Sphingolipid Metabolism

Sphingolipids are distinctly distributed across the human blood hierarchy. Modulating their metabolism preserves stem cell self-renewal.

 2

Inflammatory Stress & Stemness

Stem cells retain memory of inflammatory stress, accumulating dysfunction with age and priming clonal hematopoiesis.

 3

Targeting Leukemia Stem Cells

We translate lipid–stemness biology into novel therapeutic strategies for acute myeloid leukemia.
