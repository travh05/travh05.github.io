Research

Mapping stress
across the lifespan.

We focus on the mechanistic underpinnings of inflammatory and metabolic stress programs in human hematopoietic stem cells — and how these programs drive the risk for clonal hematopoiesis and leukemia across the human lifespan. Using primary stem cell and leukemia samples and humanized xenotransplantation models, we systematically map the cellular and molecular events that link acute and chronic inflammation to stem cell dysfunction and leukemic transformation.

1

Sphingolipid Metabolism in Stem Cells

Sphingolipids are bioactive membrane lipids whose composition shifts dramatically as blood cells differentiate.

We have discovered that the sphingolipid landscape is distinct across the human blood hierarchy, with hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) showing a unique signature. Modulating sphingolipid metabolism preserves HSC self-renewal during ex vivo expansion culture, with significant translational implications for regenerative medicine and stem cell transplantation.

Mechanistically, we have shown that sphingolipid modulation activates pro-survival proteostasis quality-control pathways — linking lipid signaling to the protein homeostasis machinery that governs stem cell durability.

Inflammatory Stress & Aging

Inflammation activates many blood cell types, driving aging and malignancy. Yet HSCs survive a lifetime of infection to sustain blood production.

We are uncovering how HSCs adapt to inflammatory stress — and how a subset of stem cells retain memory of prior inflammatory events. These memory cells accumulate with age and may seed clonal hematopoiesis, the pre-leukemic state that elevates cancer risk in older adults.

By understanding how stress imprints on stem cells across decades, we aim to identify interventions that prevent the transition from healthy aging to malignancy.

Targeting Leukemia Stem Cells

Acute myeloid leukemia is sustained by leukemia stem cells (LSCs) that resist chemotherapy and drive relapse.

We have found that modulating sphingolipid signaling — specifically through Sphingosine-1-Phosphate Receptor 3 (S1PR3) — potentiates inflammatory programs in LSCs and promotes their differentiation. This identifies a novel therapeutic axis: rather than killing LSCs directly, we coax them out of their stem-like state.

Our long-term goal is to translate these insights into clinical strategies that target LSC-specific vulnerabilities, sparing healthy hematopoiesis while eliminating the cells that drive relapse.
